I grew up fascinated by the invisible world — organisms so small you need a microscope to see them, yet powerful enough to shape the fate of entire populations. That curiosity drove me to microbiology, and the more I learned, the more I realized that bacteriophages could be one of the most powerful tools we have against the antimicrobial resistance crisis threatening global health.

S. M. Iqbal Mahamud is a microbiologist and computational biologist whose research focuses on bacteriophage biology, microbial genomics, and phage host interactions. His work spans the full pipeline of phage research, covering isolation, host-range testing, comparative genomic characterization, and therapeutic assessment. He specifically targets multidrug-resistant pathogens including Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Citrobacter freundii, and Morganella morganii, contributing to the growing body of research aimed at combating antimicrobial resistance.

With a Bachelor's degree in Microbiology from North South University, where he graduated with Distinction and Cum Laude recognition, and graduate studies in Biochemical Engineering at SIIT Thammasat University, Iqbal combines rigorous laboratory training with modern computational tools. He has published peer-reviewed research in Current Microbiology and the Journal of Advanced Biotechnology and Experimental Therapeutics, and has presented at national and international conferences in Bangladesh. He is actively seeking research collaborations in phage therapy, genomics, and microbial biotechnology.
